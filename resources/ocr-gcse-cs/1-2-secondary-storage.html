SECTION 02

Why Do We Need Secondary Storage?

To understand why secondary storage exists, you first need to understand one key property of RAM: it is volatile.

💾
RAM (Primary Storage)
Fast working memory. Holds data the CPU is actively using. But — as soon as power is removed, all data is lost.
VOLATILE
🗄️
Secondary Storage
Permanent storage. Retains data even when the device is switched off. Data survives power cuts, reboots, and years of storage.
NON-VOLATILE

Without secondary storage, every time you turned off your computer, everything would be deleted — your files, your operating system, your applications, your photos. The computer would be blank on every restart.

📝 The Three Reasons Secondary Storage Is Needed
  • Permanent storage — keeps data when power is removed (non-volatile)
  • Capacity — RAM is limited in size (typically 8–32 GB); secondary storage holds far more (hundreds of GB or even TB)
  • Cost — secondary storage is much cheaper per gigabyte than RAM — it would be impractical to replace secondary storage with RAM alone
🔗 Link to Primary Storage (1.2.1)

When RAM fills up, the operating system may temporarily move some data to secondary storage as virtual memory. This is slower than using RAM but prevents programs from crashing. This is one more reason secondary storage is essential to system operation.

✓ CHECK — Q1
A student claims: "If computers had large enough RAM, we wouldn't need secondary storage at all." What is wrong with this statement?
ANothing — larger RAM would indeed make secondary storage unnecessary for most users
BRAM is volatile — all data would still be lost every time the computer was switched off, regardless of RAM size
CRAM cannot be made any larger than its current typical size due to physical constraints
DThe CPU can only read data from secondary storage, not from RAM directly
✓ Correct. The fundamental issue is volatility — RAM loses all its contents the moment power is removed. Even with unlimited RAM capacity, you would still need non-volatile secondary storage to preserve data across power cycles. Additionally, RAM remains significantly more expensive per gigabyte.
✗ The key problem is that RAM is volatile — it loses all data when power is removed. Size is a secondary concern. Even infinite RAM would not eliminate the need for non-volatile storage, since all files, the operating system, and all data would still be lost on every shutdown.
✏️ Book Notes
  • RAM is volatile — loses data when power is off
  • Secondary storage is non-volatile — keeps data permanently
  • Three reasons needed: permanent storage, large capacity, lower cost per GB
SECTION 03

The Three Types of Secondary Storage

There are three main categories of secondary storage. Each works in a fundamentally different way.

🖴
Magnetic
HDD — Hard Disk Drive

Uses a spinning magnetic platter and a read/write head. Data is stored as magnetic patterns on the disk surface.

  • Desktop PCs
  • External backup drives
  • Data centre servers
💿
Optical
CD / DVD / Blu-ray

Uses a laser to read and write data as tiny pits and lands (bumps) on the surface of a spinning disc.

  • Music & film distribution
  • Software installation discs
  • Archival/backup media
⚡
Solid State
SSD / USB / SD Card

Uses flash memory chips — no moving parts at all. Data is stored electronically in memory cells.

  • Laptops & tablets
  • USB flash drives
  • Camera memory cards
🤔 Key Insight — Moving Parts

The most important structural difference: magnetic and optical storage both have moving mechanical parts (spinning disks, moving read heads, moving laser). Solid state has no moving parts at all. This single difference explains most of the differences in speed, durability, and noise between the types.

SECTION 04

Magnetic Storage — HDD

🖴
Hard Disk Drive (HDD)
Magnetic storage — spinning platters

A hard disk drive stores data on one or more spinning magnetic platters. A read/write head moves across the surface to read or write data as magnetic patterns. The faster the platters spin (measured in RPM), the faster data can be accessed.

✅ Advantages

  • Very high capacity — typically 1 TB to 20 TB, more for less money
  • Low cost per GB — cheapest form of large-scale storage
  • Widely available — mature, well-understood technology

❌ Disadvantages

  • Slow compared to SSD — mechanical movement limits speed
  • Fragile — moving parts can be damaged by drops or vibration
  • Heavy and bulky — poor portability compared to SSD
  • Makes noise — spinning platters produce sound and heat
📝 Best Use Cases

HDDs are ideal where large capacity at low cost is the priority and portability is not needed: desktop PC main drives, external backup drives, and large data centre storage arrays where cost per terabyte matters more than speed.

✓ CHECK — Q3
A company stores 50 TB of archive data (accessed only occasionally). They want maximum capacity at minimum cost. Which storage type and justification is most appropriate?
ASolid state drives — they are the fastest and most reliable option for important data
BMagnetic hard disk drives — they offer very high capacity at the lowest cost per GB, and access speed is less critical for archive data
COptical storage — Blu-ray discs hold more data than any other storage type
DMagnetic hard disk drives — they are faster than SSDs for large sequential file reading
✓ Correct. For archive storage — large amounts of data accessed infrequently — the key factors are capacity and cost. HDDs win on both: they provide the highest capacity at the lowest cost per GB. Since the data is rarely accessed, the slower speed compared to SSD is an acceptable trade-off.
✗ For archive data, the priorities are capacity and cost-efficiency. HDDs offer far more storage per pound than SSDs and can reach very high capacities (multiple TB per drive). Speed is less important for data that is rarely accessed. Option D is wrong — SSDs are faster than HDDs, not the other way around. Blu-ray doesn't hold more than HDDs.
✏️ Book Notes
  • HDD: spinning platters + read/write head → data as magnetic patterns
  • Advantages: very high capacity, low cost per GB
  • Disadvantages: slow, fragile (moving parts), heavy, noisy
  • Best for: large-scale backup, desktop PCs, archives
SECTION 05

Optical Storage — CD, DVD, Blu-ray

💿
Optical Disc
CD · DVD · Blu-ray

Optical storage uses a laser beam to read (and sometimes write) data. The laser detects microscopic pits (indentations) and lands (flat areas) on the reflective disc surface, which represent binary 0s and 1s.

TypeApproximate CapacityCommon Use
CD~700 MBMusic, small software
DVD~4.7 GB (single layer)Films, games, software
Blu-ray~25–50 GBHD/4K films, PS5 games

✅ Advantages

  • Very cheap per disc — CDs and DVDs cost pennies to manufacture
  • Portable and lightweight — easy to distribute physically
  • Durable if handled well — no moving parts in the disc itself

❌ Disadvantages

  • Slowest read/write speed — slower than both HDD and SSD
  • Low capacity — even Blu-ray is small compared to HDD
  • Scratches easily — surface damage destroys data permanently
  • Declining use — streaming has largely replaced physical media
📝 Best Use Cases

Optical storage is most suitable for distribution of fixed content at low cost: music CDs, film DVDs, game discs (PS5 Blu-ray), software installation discs, and low-cost archival of data that doesn't change.

✓ CHECK — Q4
A music label wants to distribute 500 copies of a new album to local shops. The album is 600 MB in size. Which storage type is most suitable, and why?
ASSD — because it is the fastest storage type and will allow shops to access tracks quickly
BHDD — because it has the highest capacity and can store many albums on one device
COptical (CD) — cheap to manufacture, the 600 MB file fits on a single CD, and CDs are easy to physically distribute in bulk
DOptical (Blu-ray) — it has the highest capacity of the optical types and is the most reliable disc format
✓ Correct. A CD holds ~700 MB, comfortably fitting the 600 MB album. CDs are extremely cheap to manufacture in bulk (pennies each), lightweight, and easy to distribute physically. Speed is not a priority for music playback. Blu-ray would be unnecessarily expensive and overpowered for this task.
✗ For bulk physical distribution of fixed content at low cost, optical media is ideal. A CD holds ~700 MB (enough for the album), costs pennies to manufacture, and is lightweight. SSDs and HDDs are far too expensive to give away per unit. Blu-ray is unnecessarily powerful — and more expensive — for 600 MB of audio data.
✏️ Book Notes
  • Optical: laser reads pits and lands on a disc surface
  • CD ~700MB, DVD ~4.7GB, Blu-ray ~25-50GB
  • Advantages: very cheap, lightweight, portable
  • Disadvantages: slowest, low capacity, scratches destroy data
SECTION 06

Solid State Storage — SSD, USB, SD Card

⚡
Solid State Storage
SSD · USB Flash Drive · SD Card

Solid state storage uses flash memory chips to store data electronically. There are no spinning discs, no read/write heads, no moving parts of any kind. Data is stored in memory cells that retain their state even without power.

🤔 Why "Solid State"?

"Solid state" means there are no moving parts — everything is done electronically in solid semiconductor material. This is the same principle as RAM and ROM chips. The difference from RAM is that flash memory is non-volatile — it keeps its data without power.

📊 Relative Read Speeds

🖴 HDD
~150 MB/s
⚡ SSD
~500–3500 MB/s
💿 Optical
~30–50 MB/s

✅ Advantages

  • Much faster than HDD — 5–20× quicker read/write speeds
  • Very durable — no moving parts means drop/shock resistant
  • Silent and runs cool — no mechanical movement
  • Highly portable — USB sticks and SD cards are tiny and lightweight

❌ Disadvantages

  • More expensive per GB — costlier than HDD for same capacity
  • Limited write cycles — flash memory cells degrade after many writes
  • Lower maximum capacity — though this gap is closing rapidly
✓ CHECK — Q5
A graphic designer uses a laptop for professional video editing, carrying it to client meetings daily. Which storage type is most appropriate for their internal laptop drive, and what is the primary reason?
AHDD — because it offers more storage capacity for large video files at lower cost
BOptical — because Blu-ray discs are the most portable format for professional work
CSSD — because it is significantly faster (helping with video editing performance), more durable when carried around, and lighter in a portable device
DHDD — because it has moving parts that make it more resilient to the stresses of daily travel
✓ Correct. An SSD wins on all three relevant factors: speed (critical for 4K video editing — rendering and reading/writing large files constantly), durability (no moving parts means drop-resistant, important for a laptop that is carried daily), and weight (lighter). The higher cost per GB is an acceptable trade-off for a professional tool.
✗ The key factors for a portable professional device are speed, durability, and weight — all of which favour SSD. HDDs have moving parts that are vulnerable to shock and vibration when being carried (Option D is wrong — moving parts make it MORE fragile, not less). Optical storage cannot serve as an internal laptop drive. SSD's higher cost is a reasonable trade-off for professional use.
✏️ Book Notes
  • SSD: flash memory chips, no moving parts
  • Advantages: fastest, very durable, silent, lightweight
  • Disadvantages: more expensive per GB than HDD
  • Best for: laptops, devices needing speed, portable storage

SECTION 08

Choosing the Right Storage — Scenarios

Exam questions will ask you to recommend a storage type for a specific context and justify your choice using the characteristics. Always link the storage properties to the specific requirements of the scenario.

📝 How to Structure Your Answer
  • Identify the most important factors for the context (speed? cost? portability?)
  • Select the storage type that best meets those factors
  • Justify using specific characteristics: "SSD is most suitable because it has faster read speeds, which means…"
  • Trade-off (for longer answers): acknowledge the disadvantage and explain why it is acceptable
🏥 Scenario A — Hospital Patient Records System
A hospital stores 80 TB of patient records. Records must be accessed quickly by doctors during appointments. Security and reliability are critical. Cost is a secondary concern. Records are not moved between locations.
✓ SCENARIO A — Q7
Which storage solution is most appropriate for this hospital system?
AOptical (Blu-ray) — extremely reliable and the data can be written once and never altered, reducing risk of corruption
BMagnetic (HDD) — it has the highest capacity per unit cost, making 80 TB affordable to store
CSolid state (SSD) — fast access speeds ensure doctors retrieve records quickly; more reliable than HDD (no moving parts); justified by medical critical use even at higher cost
DMagnetic (HDD) — spinning platters allow the hospital to access many records simultaneously through parallel read heads
✓ Correct. Speed and reliability are the priority here. SSDs are significantly faster than HDDs, meaning doctors can retrieve patient records without delay — critical in a medical setting. The lack of moving parts makes SSDs more reliable than HDDs. The higher cost per GB is justified when weighed against patient safety and uptime requirements. Option B ignores the speed requirement; Option D is technically incorrect about parallel read heads.
✗ In a hospital where speed and reliability are critical, SSD is the correct choice: fast access for real-time use, more reliable than HDD (no moving parts to fail), and stationary so portability is not needed. HDD would work but is slower and less reliable. Optical can't practically store 80 TB and is far too slow for real-time record retrieval.
🎮 Scenario B — Home User Backup Drive
A home user wants to back up 3 TB of family photos, videos, and documents. The drive will stay permanently connected to their desktop PC. They have a tight budget and access speed is not critical — backups happen automatically overnight.
✓ SCENARIO B — Q8
Which storage type is most suitable for this home backup drive, and why?
AMagnetic HDD — 3 TB at low cost, access speed is not important for an overnight backup, and the drive is stationary so fragility is less of a concern
BSSD — SSDs are more reliable than HDDs so backup data is safer on flash memory
COptical (Blu-ray) — data can be written once to a disc, protecting it from accidental deletion
DSSD — because it will need to write data every night, and SSDs handle frequent writes better than HDDs
✓ Correct. The key factors here are cost and capacity — the user has a tight budget and needs 3 TB. HDDs provide the highest capacity at the lowest cost per GB. Speed is explicitly not required (overnight backup). The drive is stationary (plugged into a desktop), so HDD's poor durability when transported is irrelevant. Option B and D misunderstand the trade-offs — while SSDs are reliable, the cost premium is not justified here. Option C: storing 3 TB on Blu-ray discs would require hundreds of discs and be impractical.
✗ For a budget home backup drive that stays on a desk and doesn't need speed, HDD wins: cheapest per GB (best for tight budget), can easily hold 3 TB, and stationary use removes the main durability risk. An SSD would cost significantly more for 3 TB with no benefit — the user doesn't need the speed advantage. Storing 3 TB on Blu-ray would require 60–120 discs and is completely impractical. Option D is factually wrong — HDDs actually handle frequent write cycles better than SSDs (which have limited write endurance).
📸 Scenario C — Wildlife Photographer
A wildlife photographer works in remote locations, often in extreme weather. They need to store up to 256 GB of RAW image files per day. Their camera uses removable storage. They carry all equipment in a backpack across rough terrain.
✓ SCENARIO C — Q9
Which storage type is most suitable for the photographer's camera storage?
AMagnetic HDD — high capacity and low cost means they can store many days of photos cheaply
BOptical (Blu-ray) — water-resistant and easy to swap between camera and laptop in the field
CSolid state (SD card / SSD) — durable (no moving parts survive rough terrain and weather), portable (small and light), and fast enough for rapid RAW file capture
DMagnetic HDD — external hard drives can be used as removable camera storage with an adapter
✓ Correct. The dominant factors here are durability and portability. SD cards and compact SSDs have no moving parts — they survive drops, vibration, rain, dust, and rough terrain where an HDD would almost certainly fail. They are also tiny and lightweight (critical when hiking with a backpack), and fast enough to handle continuous RAW image writing. The higher cost per GB is justified for a professional tool used in harsh environments.
✗ For a photographer in rough terrain, durability and portability are critical. SD cards (solid state) have no moving parts — they survive drops, vibration, and weather that would destroy an HDD. HDDs are fragile when moving (moving parts fail on impact) and far too bulky for camera use. Optical discs cannot be used in cameras and are fragile. Solid state is the clear answer whenever portability and durability in demanding conditions are required.
✏️ Book Notes
  • Speed critical + stationary → SSD (e.g. database, hospital, gaming PC)
  • Max capacity, low cost, stationary → HDD (e.g. home backup, server archive)
  • Portability + durability in field → Solid state (e.g. laptop, camera, USB)
  • Cheap physical distribution of fixed content → Optical (e.g. music CD, game disc)

🔗 The Big Idea

Every storage decision is a trade-off. The fastest storage (SSD) is the most expensive. The cheapest (HDD) is the slowest and most fragile. The most portable (USB stick) has the smallest capacity. Understanding these trade-offs — and linking them to the specific needs of a scenario — is what earns marks in the exam.

FINAL — Q10
State two reasons why computers need secondary storage in addition to RAM.
ARAM is too fast for most tasks; secondary storage slows down the system to a usable speed
BRAM is volatile (data is lost when power is removed) and has limited capacity; secondary storage is non-volatile and can hold far more data at lower cost
CThe CPU can only directly read from secondary storage, not from RAM

✓ Correct. The OS, applications, and user data all require this permanent, high-capacity storage.

Option A is wrong — speed is not the reason. Option C is wrong — the CPU reads directly from RAM. Option D is backwards — the OS is stored on secondary storage and loaded into RAM.

Which characteristic explains why a solid state drive is more suitable than a magnetic hard disk drive for use inside a laptop that is frequently carried in a bag?
ACapacity — SSDs hold more data than HDDs, making laptops more useful on the move
BDurability — SSDs have no moving parts, making them resistant to damage from the vibration and impact of being carried, whereas HDD platters can be damaged by movement

HDDs have spinning platters and a delicate read/write head that can be damaged or cause data corruption if the drive is moved, bumped, or dropped while operating. SSDs have no moving parts — they are fundamentally more resistant to the physical stresses of being carried in a bag. Speed is a bonus, but durability is the direct answer to the "frequently carried" context.

SSDs have no moving parts and can withstand being carried without data loss. Option A is wrong — HDDs can actually have higher capacity. Option C is wrong — SSDs are more expensive than HDDs. A streaming service stores 500 TB of 4K films on their servers. The films are accessed by millions of users simultaneously at all hours. Which storage type is most appropriate, justified by two characteristics?

✓ Correct. This is a nuanced real-world answer that many streaming services actually use. 500 TB of content requires HDD-level cost efficiency and capacity. A tiered architecture — SSDs for hot/popular content, HDDs for the cold archive — addresses both constraints. This shows sophisticated application of the characteristics. Option B is wrong to dismiss speed — millions of simultaneous streams absolutely require fast storage.

But millions of simultaneous streams require fast read speeds that HDD alone can't provide efficiently. FINAL — Q14
Explain why optical storage has the lowest read speed of the three storage types. [2 marks]
AOptical discs are made of plastic which absorbs laser energy, slowing down data reading
BThe laser used in optical drives can only read data in one direction, requiring multiple passes over the disc
COptical drives have a physically spinning disc and a laser mechanism that must move to the correct track — both of these mechanical processes introduce latency that fundamentally limits speed compared to electronic (SSD) or faster magnetic (HDD) storage
DOptical discs store less data than other types, so the read speed is proportionally lower
✓ Correct — and this is a 2-mark style answer. The two reasons: (1) the disc must spin mechanically to bring the correct data under the laser — spinning speed limits how quickly sequential data can be read; (2) the laser read head must physically move (seek) across the disc to find data — this mechanical seek time adds latency. Both are consequences of having mechanical moving parts, just as HDDs suffer from similar (though less severe) limitations.
✗ Optical drives are slow because of their mechanical components: (1) the disc must spin and the laser must be in the right position before data can be read — this physical movement takes time; (2) the laser head must seek across the disc surface, creating seek latency. Neither the material of the disc nor the direction of reading is the cause. Small capacity doesn't cause slow speed — the two characteristics are independent.
